about our project



Hi! We’re Andrea and Alex, two students at NYU. For a web design class, our final project was supposed to be Internet art, but we wanted to do something a little different. Since we’ve been studying in Shanghai, our boba habits have gone a bit haywire since it’s readily available pretty much everywhere we go or through ele.me, a delivery app, and it’s much cheaper than in the US.

After seeing Krist Wongsuphasawat’s “Boba Science,” which visualizes the optimized sip of bubble tea such that you can finish your tea and boba at the same time, we wanted to know more about other people’s boba buying and drinking habits so we created a Google form to assess where in the world people are from, where they went to university if at all, their age, how often they get boba, where they get boba, and how they customize their drink. Moreover, we wanted to take all that information and present it in a fun and engaging way.

Special thanks to Subtle Asian Traits for making up the bulk of our survey pool and for providing some entertaining and much needed information, like what your boba order says about you or just how many boba shops there are all over the world (and how they rank against each other).

Thanks so much for much for visiting our site, and have a bobalicious day! (ﾉ´ヮ`)ﾉ*:･ﾟ

what is boba?

Boba. Bubble tea. Pearl milk tea. 珍珠奶茶 (zhēnzhū nǎichá). Traditionally speaking, it’s a tea-based beverage invented in Taiwan in the 1980s. Made of a black, green or oolong tea base with milk and an optional helping of sugar and a good shaking to best combine the flavors, the drink is a cool (or hot, if that’s more your style), refreshing and, in our opinion, more fun alternative to coffee thanks to the plethora of toppings you can add. Boba’s most iconic topping is, well, boba: chewy, black tapioca balls that are first boiled then soaked in either a honey or brown sugar solution for added flavor. Other popular toppings include mini boba, grass jelly, red bean, egg pudding, rainbow jelly and salted cheese, to name a few.

What makes the milk tea base so unique is that you can customize it with flavors, including (but not limited to) Yakult (a Japanese yogurt drink popularized by Netflix’s To All the Boys I’ve Loved Before), taro, honey, chocolate, Okinawa (brown sugar) and more fruity flavors like strawberry, mango and litchi. If tea isn’t your thing, no worries! Fruit-flavored slushes are also offered at many bubble tea shops, making bubble tea – and we use this term very loosely, given all it can encompass – a drink for all seasons. But how did this delicious and versatile alternative to coffee and all-around lifestyle for some first come about?

history of boba ヽ(°〇°)ﾉ

Though there’s still a lot of speculation as to who came up with the delicious and frothy beverage, most credit Liu Han-Chieh and Lin Hsiu Hui of Chun Shui Tang teashop in Taichung, Taiwan with the invention of bubble tea. In the early 1980s, Liu Han-Chieh first got the idea of serving Chinese tea cold after a visit to Japan brought to his attention how the Japanese served coffee cold. In 1988, Chun Shui Tang experimented by adding a sweetened Taiwanese tapioca pudding into her iced Assam tea and once she introduced it to the menu, it quickly outsold all the shop’s other iced teas.

As word of the new drink spread, it caught on with other teashops that started using fruit powders and syrups (as a cheaper and more durable alternative to fruit) for added flavor and expanding their available toppings to include grass jelly, almond jelly, egg pudding, red bean and more. Finally, many shops switched from milk to non-dairy creamer or milk powder for their longer shelf-life. The tea and milk were then shaken together to give the drink a frothy and creamy consistency, as well as the name “bubble” tea for the bubbles that surfaced after the drink was mixed.

Since its creation, bubble tea has been a massive part of Taiwanese culture, but it wasn’t until the 1990s that Taiwanese immigration to the US and growing cafe culture led to a bubble tea craze in America. Growing up, our generation frequented shops like Quickly, Tapioca Express and more regional options for a quick drink or a weekend pick-me-up with a side of popcorn chicken. The bubble tea craze started dying down in the 2000s only to be revived with the opening of Boba Guys, which offered high-quality teas, laid-back atmosphere and real milk. Since then, bubble tea culture has seen a resurgence all over the world, especially where diasporic Asian communities reside, and have become an iconic symbol of Asian culture.

boba preferences across the globe

We surveyed over 250 people to see what boba preferences across the world were like. In the map above, we’ve plotted every person who responded to our survey and customized their data point to their boba order, and the points are color-coded by age group. The orange points are under 18; the red points, which make up the majority of the points on the map, range from 18 to 25; the purple points range from 26 to 33; the yellow points range from 34 to 40; the light pink points are 40 and over.

age demographics

6.9% of participants were under 18, 79.7% were between 18 and 25, 8.3% were between 26 and 33, 2.2% were between 34 and 40, and 2.9% were 40 and over.

boba buying frequency

We were a bit surprised that 38.8% of our respondents only got boba once a week (you can’t fool us!). According to the data we gathered, the average participant consumes 2.44 cups of boba per week – which is a pretty sound habit in our opinion – and 1.4% of participants (4 individuals) drink 10 or more cups of boba per week (no shame, just awe)!

tea preferences

35.1% of participants favored a black tea base over all other options, but green tea was a close second with 31.5%. Oolong tea followed with 15.6%, with Thai tea (7.2%) and matcha (6.9%) not too far behind. The remaining 3.7% were divided among earl grey, pu-erh, jasmine, brown rice and fruit tea base, and to our sadness, only one person chose a slush/snow over a tea as their base preference.

sugar & ice preferences

Typical sugar & ice percentages offered are (0%) no added sugar/ice; (25%) light sugar/ice; (50%) half sugar/ice; (75%) less sugar/ice (100%) full sugar/ice; and (125) extra sugar/ice. The percentages in the graph are represented in decimals. Sugar is plotted along the X-axis and ice is plotted along the Y-axis. The size of each bubble represents the number of respondents who selected the corresponding sugar and ice combination. For example, 45 of our respondents chose 50% sugar / 50% ice.

milk preferences

In spite of the (often true) stereotype that Asians are lactose intolerant, 69.6% of our survey pool preferred their milk tea with actual milk, and 17.4% preferred fruit teas over milk teas. 12.3% favored dairy-free creamers or lactose-free alternatives like nut, soy and oat milks, and 0.8% listed “Other” options that we’re not entirely sure classify as milk preferences (cheese, anybody?).

topping preferences

Though 9.4% of respondents opted to savor milk or fruit tea without toppings, the remaining 90.6% selected boba to be one of their many toppings. Grass jelly (18.1%) and lychee jelly (17.8%) were also favorites among the 21 toppings that we listed, and participants were free to add their own if they weren’t on the list. Included among these other toppings were grape jelly, oats and chia seeds which, pro tip, are just basil seeds in disguise since basil seeds are cheaper – but you didn’t hear it from us.

flavor preferences

While 43.8% of participants favored milk tea’s original flavor, 56.2% liked to jazz their orders up a bit more. Taro was the most popular, with 8.3% of participants selecting it as their preference, followed by honey (6.9%) and Okinawa (6.5%), better known as "dirty tea" for its swirls of brown sugar syrup. Not to be forgotten are mango (6.2%), passion fruit (4.3%), rose (4.3%), peach (3.6%), wintermelon (3.3%), lychee/litchi (3.3%), Yakult (2.2%), grapefruit (1.1%), toffee/caramel (1.1%), banana (0.4%), and hazelnut (0.4%).
